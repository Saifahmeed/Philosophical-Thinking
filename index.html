Exploring Heidegger's Philosophy

Developed by Saif Abd Elfattah

Introduction

I will explore three fundamental questions:

  1. What is the difference between thinking and scientific measurement?
  2. How should thinking take into consideration the fourfold of human being (Earth, Sky, Divinities, and Mortals)?
  3. In what ways is thinking, grounded in the fourfold of our being, manifested in aspects of life such as professional, personal, spiritual, or artistic experiences?

By analyzing clips from thought-provoking videos and movies, I aim to unravel how Heidegger’s ideas challenge the modern reliance on calculative reasoning, instead urging us to embrace a deeper, more meaningful mode of thinking. Drawing connections between Heidegger’s philosophy, cultural expressions like Miyazaki’s films, and my own experiences, I will show how the interplay of the fourfold of our being can ground us in a more holistic understanding of life.

Understanding the Essence of Technology

Timing: 1:08 to 1:57

This clip delves into Heidegger’s critique of the instrumental view of technology. As described in the video, “this is what Heidegger calls the anthropological or instrumental view of technology, and it’s trivially true,” this perspective reduces technology to being just a tool for achieving goals. While not incorrect, Heidegger believes this viewpoint is shallow because it overlooks the essence of technology—how it profoundly shapes human existence and our connection to the world.
The video explains that this instrumental perspective focuses solely on what technology can do, ignoring its broader implications. Heidegger challenges us to go beyond evaluating technology’s functionality and instead reflect on how it transforms the way we see and engage with reality. By doing so, we uncover the deeper impact of technology, urging us to think critically not only about its usefulness but also its influence on defining the human experience.

Timing: 3:27 to 4:49

In this clip, the video delves into Heidegger’s concept of the essence of technology as a mode of revealing. For Heidegger, technology is not just about tools but a way of uncovering or bringing forth truth.
The example of the lake illustrates how different contexts reveal the same object differently. A medieval peasant might see the lake as a place of rest, while a modern industrialist might see it as a potential site for a hydraulic plant. This shift in perception demonstrates that revealing is not objective but deeply shaped by one's worldview and purpose and how technology can change our preception.
Heidegger introduces "enframing" as the technological mode of revealing. Enframing presents everything as standing reserve—resources ready for use. Under this mode, the value of things is determined by their utility. This perspective influences not just how we see the world but how we act, prioritize, and define meaning, emphasizing efficiency and control over intrinsic value. The clip challenges us to consider how this technological framing shapes modern life and our sense of what truly matters.

Timing: 5:43 to 7:23

In this clip, the video highlights Heidegger’s critique of "enframing," a concept central to his understanding of technology's essence. The quote, “nature becomes a gigantic gasoline station, an energy source for modern technology and industry,” encapsulates his concern that under enframing, the world is reduced to a mere resource—standing reserve—ready for exploitation and calculation.
Heidegger doesn’t reject technology or science outright, nor does he advocate for primitivism. Instead, his critique focuses on how enframing dominates modern life, shaping not only how we see the world but also how we think and act. It creates the illusion that its way of revealing the world—through efficiency, utility, and control—is the only valid perspective. This mindset marginalizes other ways of experiencing the world, such as seeing it poetically or appreciating its inherent beauty.
The video’s connection to Miyazaki’s films is a brilliant way to illustrate Heidegger’s point. Miyazaki’s movies often reveal the world as something sacred, full of wonder and intrinsic value, rather than as a resource to be exploited. Heidegger would likely see this as an alternative mode of revealing, one that reminds us of what enframing tends to obscure: the richness and diversity of ways to experience and relate to the world. This perspective isn’t about abandoning technology but about rethinking how we engage with it and ensuring it doesn’t overshadow other, deeper dimensions of existence.

Timing: 7:23 to 9:31

In this clip, the video dives into Heidegger’s idea of challenging forth—how modern technology forces nature into being something it’s not. As the quote says, “technology coerces nature...it forces it to be transformed into mere resources,” this means technology doesn’t just use nature—it dominates and exploits it, stripping away its essence and treating it as nothing more than raw material.
Heidegger contrasts this with bringing forth, an approach rooted in respect and care. Imagine an artisan carving a violin from wood. They don’t fight against the wood’s nature; instead, they work with it, bringing out its natural beauty—its texture, imperfections, and unique qualities. There’s a partnership between the artisan and the material, where each shapes the other in a way that respects what the material inherently is.
Modern technology, on the other hand, doesn’t care about this harmony. It demands, extracts, and exploits, often to the point of destruction—like cutting down forests faster than they can regrow or overfeeding animals until they can’t move. Heidegger sees this as a dangerous mindset, where nature is treated as nothing but a resource to be used up.
This is why Heidegger and the video connect so strongly to the craftsmanship often shown in Miyazaki’s films. Miyazaki’s stories celebrate the beauty of working with your hands and respecting the world around you. They remind us of a time when people worked with nature rather than against it, showing us how much more meaningful and fulfilling that relationship can be. For Heidegger, this isn’t about rejecting technology but about learning to engage with the world in a way that honors its essence rather than ignoring it.

Timing: 11:04 to 11:41

In this clip, the video draws a thoughtful parallel between Miyazaki’s careful use of technology in filmmaking and Heidegger’s philosophy. The quote, “he always insists on drawing by hand,” reflects Miyazaki’s commitment to preserving the human touch in his work. He is wary of how technology, when overused, can shift the creative process towards efficiency, often at the cost of authenticity and emotional expression.
This echoes Heidegger’s own refusal to use a typewriter. Heidegger believed that a typewriter homogenizes writing—it removes the personal mark of the writer’s hand, stripping away the individuality and human connection that handwritten work carries. For both Miyazaki and Heidegger, the concern isn’t technology itself but how it can change the way we experience the world, often prioritizing speed over depth and uniqueness.
Miyazaki’s insistence on drawing by hand is a resistance to this trend. It’s a reminder that some forms of creation demand the personal touch—the imperfections, the quirks, and the time it takes to make something truly meaningful. In a world increasingly driven by technological optimization, both Heidegger and Miyazaki remind us of the value in slowing down, staying connected to the process, and preserving what makes our work—and ourselves—unique.

Timing: 17:00 to 18:26

In this clip, the video discusses Heidegger’s idea of how modern technology transforms the way we see and interact with the earth. The quote, “the earth now reveals itself as a coal mining district, the soil is a mineral deposit,” shows how technology reduces the natural world to a collection of resources to be exploited. What was once land that peasants carefully tended, respecting its natural processes, has now become something to challenge and extract from.
Heidegger contrasts this with the older practice of bringing forth, where the work of farming involved cooperating with nature—planting seeds and trusting in the forces of growth. This approach respected the land’s rhythms and essence, treating it as something to care for, not dominate. Today, agriculture itself has become part of what Heidegger calls challenging forth, where nature is forced into submission, becoming a mechanized food industry driven by efficiency and output. Even the air and earth are seen as resources to be mined, processed, or manipulated.
The video’s connection to Princess Mononoke beautifully illustrates this tension. Miyazaki’s film portrays the clash between traditional ways of living and the rise of industrialization, where nature is stripped of its intrinsic value and turned into something to be extracted and used. As the official website explains, the movie reflects the early stages of the seemingly endless conflict between the natural world and modern industrial civilization.
Both Heidegger and Miyazaki remind us that this shift in perspective comes at a cost. When we reduce the earth to a resource, we lose our connection to its beauty and its deeper meaning. Their work encourages us to rethink our relationship with nature, not as something to conquer, but as something to care for and coexist with.

The Fourfold of Human Existence

Timing: 21:28 to 28:14

This clip dives deep into Heidegger’s idea of dwelling—living meaningfully by being rooted in the world around us. For Heidegger, life isn’t just about existing; it’s about being connected to the Earth, the Sky, Mortals, and Divinities, the fourfold that shapes everything we do. The quote, “we are increasingly surrounded by places completely devoid of any meaning,” captures the emptiness of modern life, filled with repetitive spaces like malls and office buildings that feel disconnected and sterile.
Heidegger believes that we’ve lost something vital in modernity: a sense of connection and reverence. We cover the Earth, pollute the Sky, deny our mortality, and forget the sacred. By trying to conquer everything—turning life into a series of calculations and optimizations—we sap it of mystery and meaning. For Heidegger, acknowledging our limits, including the fact that we’ll one day die, is what gives life its depth. Without this, our actions can feel empty.
The clip ties this beautifully to Miyazaki’s films. In Castle in the Sky, the disconnect from the Earth and Sky is literal: the floating city of Laputa falls apart because it’s lost its connection to the natural world. Miyazaki’s work reminds us of the beauty in places that feel lived-in and meaningful.
Heidegger’s fourfold offers a guide back to meaningful living. The Earth grounds us, providing food, shelter, and a sense of home. The Sky brings rhythm and order to our days and seasons. Mortals remind us of our finiteness and the importance of cherishing our time. And Divinities point to the mystery of life—something beyond our ability to control or fully understand.
Through his films, Miyazaki captures this mystery. Influenced by Shinto, he shows us worlds where every leaf, stone, and breeze has meaning. Heidegger and Miyazaki both remind us that to truly live, we need to dwell—not by dominating the world but by caring for it and finding wonder in its beauty and complexity.

Timing: 28:57 to 30:41

This video connects Heidegger’s ideas about divinities with Shinto practices, as often depicted in Miyazaki’s films. In Shinto, the sacred isn’t something far away or beyond reach—it’s part of everyday life and the natural world. A quote from the video sums it up well: “The spirits in Shinto are something imminent to the everyday.”
In Shinto, sacredness is tied to specific places, like a shrine, a tree, or a mountain. The place itself is what makes it special. This is different from Christian churches, which are often sacred because they symbolize a connection to a higher, spiritual realm. Shinto shrines, however, are always closely connected to the natural beauty around them. As Sokyo Ono explains, “Shrines cannot be separated from the natural beauty that traditionally surrounds them.”
This view of sacredness being rooted in nature matches Heidegger’s philosophy. For Heidegger, divinities aren’t about trying to understand or control the unknown—they’re about feeling awe and reverence for it. As Thomas Sakulis puts it, Shinto encourages people to feel “at home with awe,” to embrace the mystery of life rather than trying to dominate it.
A simple act like offering rice at a shrine ties into Heidegger’s idea of the “fourfold.” The Earth provides the soil for the rice, the Sky brings rain and weather, the Mortal (a person) offers the rice, and the Divinity blesses the act. This unity reflects Heidegger’s idea of “dwelling,” where life has meaning because of its connection to the Earth, Sky, Mortals, and Divinities.
Miyazaki’s films bring this philosophy to life. His stories are filled with spirits, shrines, and nature, where the sacred is woven into everyday life. They remind us to live with care and reverence, appreciating the places and beauty around us. Both Heidegger and Shinto teach us that life’s meaning comes not from trying to control everything but from cherishing the connections and mysteries that make life complete.

Relating Heidegger's Philosophy to Life in Egypt

Life in a village

As someone who live in Nasr City, I feel disconnected from nature. I would prefer to live in an Egyptian village. This video beautifully portrays the essence of a traditional Egyptian village, offering a glimpse into a way of life deeply connected to nature. It invites us to ponder the profound bond between humans and the natural world—a relationship that philosopher Martin Heidegger described as the "fourfold" of human existence: earth, sky, divinities, and mortals.
In this peaceful village, life is lived in harmony with these elements. The earth is symbolized by the fields and rivers, providing both sustenance and beauty. The fertile soil supports a simple, agricultural lifestyle, encouraging gratitude and care for the land. Unlike urban areas like Nasr City or Fifth Settlement, where green spaces are limited and often disconnected, the village weaves nature seamlessly into daily life, fostering a deep and tangible connection with the earth.
The sky here feels closer—both literally and figuratively. Modest homes, typically one or two stories tall, maintain a sense of connection with the open sky. Without skyscrapers obstructing the view, the horizon appears infinite, promoting freedom and introspection. This starkly contrasts with urban landscapes, where towering buildings not only block the sky but also create a sense of detachment from nature.
Divinity is reflected in the tranquil rhythm of village life. Living simply amid nature allows space for reflection, prayer, and spiritual connection. The slower pace, free from constant technological distractions, gives residents time to contemplate their place in the universe. Urban life, with its relentless pace and overstimulation, often makes such moments of introspection rare.
The people—mortals—are also more connected to each other in the village. Narrow streets and smaller homes naturally foster a close-knit community, where neighbors interact frequently and support one another. This is a sharp contrast to cities, where multi-story buildings and isolated living spaces can lead to social disconnection. In urban areas, neighbors often live side by side without ever truly knowing each other, as city living tends to prioritize privacy over community.
The video also highlights how the village's minimal reliance on cars and technology enhances both human and environmental bonds. Fewer vehicles mean cleaner air, quieter streets, and safer spaces for pedestrians. Children can play outside freely, and neighbors can gather without the constant noise of traffic. This kind of lifestyle reduces stress and creates a sense of belonging that is increasingly rare in urban environments.
Ultimately, this village exemplifies a way of life that harmonizes with the "fourfold" of human existence, balancing the natural and human-made worlds. It offers a stark contrast to urban life, where sprawling developments, tall buildings, and technology often distance us from the earth, sky, and each other. Watching this video reminds us of the value of simplicity, community, and our intrinsic connection to nature. Perhaps it’s time to consider how we can incorporate aspects of this village life into modern cities, creating environments that honor the fourfold and nurture the human spirit.
